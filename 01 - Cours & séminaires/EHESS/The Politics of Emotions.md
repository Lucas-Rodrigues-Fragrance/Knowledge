### c) The “Compass of Shame”: four patterned responses

Nathanson’s most famous contribution is the **Compass of Shame**, which describes four main ways people cope with shame:

1. **Withdrawal** – retreating, hiding, or disappearing.
    
2. **Attack Self** – self-criticism, self-punishment, internalizing the shame.
    
3. **Avoidance** – distraction, denial, compulsive behaviors to escape the feeling (e.g., addictions, fame-seeking, humor).
    
4. **Attack Other** – projecting shame outward, humiliating or blaming others.
    

Each person tends to develop habitual “scripts” of shame response, depending on personality and past experiences.

I. Core Academic Proposition and Historical Thesis

The central thesis posits that the **Nuremberg Trials in 1945** marked the emergence of an international legal order that profoundly transformed the political and moral culture of many democracies, with cultural change becoming evident around the 1970s.

This massive cultural shift is proposed to be analyzed through two foundational concepts:

1. **Guilt (La Gueule):** This represents the **memorial process** and cultural memory, notably the memory of the Holocaust and its relationship to other memorial cultures, such as the memory of colonialism. This collective memory is organized around the notion of a past crime.

2. **Hate/Shame (La Haine):** This emotion provides a prism for understanding **identity politics**, which the instructor defines not merely as identity-based politics, but as a political form addressing the humiliation or hate directed towards members of a group. The movement's starting point is traced back to the 1972 Combahee River Statement, where the refusal to be disappointed in oneself is a key theme, linking the refusal of hate to the culture of hate.

The instructor frames this inquiry by comparing two crucial historical moments in Germany: the deep sense of **humiliation and resentment** caused by the "war guilt clause" (Clause 231) of the 1919 Treaty of Versailles, and the dramatic cultural and moral change symbolized by **Willy Brandt kneeling at the Warsaw Ghetto Memorial in 1970**. This gesture demonstrated a fundamental societal shift in which a head of state entirely embraced the notion of collective guilt.

II. Sociological Methodology: The Emotions and the Social

The seminar adopts the **Sociology of Emotions** as its core analytical framework. This approach, required to transform emotions from individual psychological experiences into objects of sociological study, involves two primary operations:

1. **Social Scripting of Emotional Content:** This operation aims to demonstrate that emotional experience is not solely individual, but relies heavily on collective and social scripts. Examples of scripting provided are:

    ◦ **Gender:** Illustrated by the differing emotional reactions of men and women to external stimuli (e.g., an animal in a bar).

    ◦ **Class:** Emotional control (e.g., control of envy) being central to the education of middle and upper classes, varying significantly by cultural zone (e.g., more pronounced in Scandinavian/Germanic countries than Latin ones).

    ◦ **Culture of Honour:** In honor cultures (like traditional Arab culture), an individual's honor is externally dependent on the public perception of the group, particularly the ability of men to protect the virginity of women they "possess". This stands in stark contrast to modern, abstract notions of honor (e.g., not lying or betraying).

2. **Emotions as Collective Phenomena:** Emotions aid in organizing and generating collective actions. The instructor cites the 2011 protests in Tunisia (Arab Spring), sparked by the self-immolation of Mohamed Bouazizi, whose emotional outburst (anger and hate stemming from humiliation by a female officer) spread through social media, leading to mass protests and political regime change. This process is termed the "circulation of affects through social links" (contagion).

The overarching sociological goal is to study the relationship between **institutional ideas** (specifically moral concepts and those emanating from international law/organizations) and **emotions**. The instructor tentatively identifies a potentially new sociological domain: **the impact of international ideas on democracies**.

III. Conceptualizing Guilt and Shame/Hate

The instructor places both **Guilt** and **Hate/Shame** within the category of **self-conscious emotions** (or reflexive emotions). These emotions are cognitively complex, developing later in childhood, and require a sophisticated apparatus involving **self-evaluation** and the ability to **see oneself through the eyes of another**.

A central requirement for these self-conscious emotions is a **well-organized conception of agency**, meaning the individual perceives themselves as the author of their actions. The legal distinction between **responsibility** (being held accountable, as in an accidental death) and **guilt** (implying intent or a connection to a wrong action) is crucial here.

The instructor offers a specific distinction between the two emotions for the purpose of the seminar:

• **Shame/Hate (Haine):** Primarily concerns **one's image** and how one is perceived or diminished by others (i.e., social survival).

• **Guilt (Gueule):** Concerns a **specific, reprehensible behaviour** or the violation of an internalized moral norm (i.e., reflecting on being an "horrible person").

IV. Critique of the Public Sphere

A key intervention in the course involves rejecting the traditional Habermasian normative theory of the public sphere as a space dominated by rational-communicative discussion.

The instructor argues that the contemporary public sphere, contrary to Habermas's ideal, is saturated with ideas heavily charged with emotions. Evidence includes:

2. **Media Ecology and Attention Economy:** News analysis shows content is often affectively balanced (positive/hope or negative/fear) to increase readership. The shift to social media exacerbates this, as attention becomes the primary currency; therefore, emotion is the main way to attract and anchor attention, making the Habermasian model an ideal rather than a reality.

3. **Circulation of Affected Ideas:** The seminar will instead treat the public sphere as a location for the **circulation of affected ideas** (ideas affectives).

The instructor also raises crucial questions regarding the **scale** of the public sphere (national, global, transnational), citing global platforms like Substack and the transnational impact of media like Al Jazeera, which can act against the interests of national leaders.

The seminar emphasizes the importance of understanding **emotional encouragement** (l'encouragement émotionnel) for ideas to circulate better **transnationally**.
