### Models of Artificial Intelligence: A Philosophical Interrogation

This course, led by **Thomas Souverain** (a post-doctoral researcher at CEA with a PhD in explaining AI models, particularly in finance and employment discrimination), explores the intricate relationship between Artificial Intelligence and philosophy. The course aims to precisely define AI models and subject them to philosophical inquiry, addressing questions that statisticians might not. The pedagogical approach is inclusive, welcoming students from diverse backgrounds, including non-mathematicians and non-philosophers.

**Course Structure & Key Themes:**

The course is structured into several thematic groups, progressing from foundational definitions to complex ethical and anthropological questions.

1. **Defining AI Models (First 3 Sessions):**
    - **Historical Context:** An exploration of the history of ideas, linking AI models to the concept of **automata**.
    - **Contemporary AI:** Introduction to modern AI models, specifically **machine learning** and deep learning (e.g., neural networks, transformers), and the challenge of **explainability** ("black box" problem).

4. **Anthropological Questions (Final 3 Sessions):**
    - **The Nature of AI:** Deep philosophical questions: **"Does AI think?"** and **"Does AI speak?"**.
    - **Impact on Human Being:** How AI changes our understanding of what it means to be human, especially as we share "rationality" with machines. The course emphasizes that while robots are relevant, the core focus is on the underlying "automatisms" or models.

**Deep Dive: Automata and the Mechanization of Man (First Session Focus)**

The initial sessions delve into the history of automata, defining it through three key aspects:

1. **The Effect of Illusion:** Automata create the belief in life (e.g., Homer's golden maidens "seem alive," "go straight to the goal"). This creates a sense of "human hubris" in creating life.
2. **Substitution of Labor:** Automata are designed to **replace human (or divine) labor**. This extends beyond physical tasks to **mental labour, specifically calculation**, where humans are prone to error.
3. **Intrinsic Qualities/Learning:** Automata possess "reason" and "habits," implying an internal, thought-driven movement, as suggested by the Indo-European etymology of _automatos_ (self-moving, movement produced by thought).

Historically, the project of automata emerged from two main sources:

- **Mechanics:** Focused on practical ends, using "cunning" (ruse) with nature to harness and redirect its forces (e.g., Hero of Alexandria's mechanical birds, canals). This represents the **material realization** of the automaton project.
- **Magic:** Seeking "secrets of nature" or "seminar reasons" (germs of beings) to produce effects, initially involving invocation of demons (e.g., in Augustine). Later, during the Renaissance, this evolved into "natural magic," seeking hidden "sympathies" and "antipathies" in nature without demonic intervention. This convergence led to the emergence of the **"expert" figure** who investigates nature's secrets.

Throughout history, **two coexisting attitudes** toward creating automata have been observed:

- **Apeirophilic (Respectful/Contemplative):** An attitude of reverence towards nature, refraining from creating automata.
- **Promethean (Forcing Nature):** An attitude of actively seeking to force nature's secrets and replicate life/reason, using all available means (mechanics, magic, physics).

**Roger Bacon** (13th-century theologian) exemplifies the Promethean attitude by developing optical lenses and correcting the solar calendar, while also advocating for mechanical inventions to inspire awe for God, distinguishing mechanical ingenuity from magic. This marked a turn towards modernity, rejecting reliance on authority in favour of rational inquiry.

The drive to mechanize calculation became particularly pronounced in the 19th century due to increased needs for precision in maritime transport and astronomy, alongside advancements from the Industrial Revolution. **Charles Babbage's analytical engine**, a universal calculator, was inspired by **Jacquard's punched-card loom** (which automated complex weaving patterns). Babbage applied this concept to calculation, coding machine states with 20,000 cards. His collaborator, **Ada Lovelace**, described this machine and developed what is considered the **first algorithm**, recognizing its ability to manipulate symbols beyond mere numbers, akin to how the Jacquard loom "weaves flowers and motifs". Lovelace lucidly understood that while calculating machines existed before (e.g., Pascaline), Babbage's machine represented a higher degree of capability for performing any operation, marking a significant difference.

Comprehensive Summary of the Philosophy of Artificial Intelligence Models Course

This course, hosted by the **University of Ottawa** [1], focuses on the **philosophy of artificial intelligence (IA) models** [2]. Its primary objective is to introduce IA models and demonstrate that they are legitimate **objects of philosophical study** [3]. This study encompasses various issues, including **epistemological problems** (philosophies of knowledge, description, and explanation), as well as concerns of **equity, aesthetics, and philosophical anthropology** [3].

The course progresses from a historical survey of automata to a detailed analysis of modern AI models and the core philosophical tensions surrounding them.

I. Historical Context: From Automata to Mechanization (Antiquity to Turing)

The course began by broadly surveying the history of automata, from **Greek antiquity** (or archaic periods) up to the **Turing machine** [2]. This history illustrated two parallel mechanizations:

1. **Mechanization of Movement:** This involves creating systems, like irrigation, that "trick" nature to accomplish tasks, thus relieving humans of labour and physical movement [4]. Examples include the fantastical serving women of Hephaestus or ancient rolling tripods [5, 6].

2. **Mechanization of Calculation:** This involves using operators or systems to perform mental calculation, relieving the brain of systematic mental effort [4, 6].

The **principal thesis** of the course is that there has been a **progressive slide** from the _fantasy of the automaton_ to the **realization of this idea at the level of thought** (the act of thinking) through specific mechanization, particularly formal neural networks [7].

The history of calculation culminated in the **Turing machine**, which was a key logical foundation. Turing showed that numbers and propositions could be manipulated by a machine with memory and a reading tape, representing a mechanized form of reasoning [8, 9]. Crucially, the move from older, purely mechanical machines (like Babbage's steam-powered computer) required a shift to **electricity, chips, and electrical relays** to make logical procedures materializable [10, 11].

II. The Prehistory of AI: Cybernetics (1943)

The immediate prehistory of the AI project came from the intellectual movement known as **Cybernetics**, emerging around 1943 during World War II [12, 13].

Cybernetics uniquely associated the **mechanization of thought and movement** [12, 14]:

1. Wiener and Bigelow: The Feedback Loop (Instrumentalism)

• **Context:** Working on **anti-aircraft defense** during WWII [13].

• **Concept:** They developed a **probabilistic theory** to predict a target's (an airplane's) future position based on partial current information [15]. This prediction is continuously **corrected** by real-time data on the aircraft's actual trajectory—a mechanism called a **feedback loop** (_boucle de rétroaction_) [15-17].

• **Analogy to Thought:** They drew a parallel between this mechanized physical movement (the aircraft correction) and **voluntary human movements** (like lifting a glass) that are "inhabited by reason" [18, 19].

• **Philosophical Stance:** This approach is rooted in **instrumentalism**—using an analogy to achieve an effective result (like air defense) [20, 21].

2. McCulloch and Pitts: The Network of Propositions (Realism)

• **McCulloch** (psychiatrist) and **Pitts** (mathematician/logician) sought to find the **logical and material mechanisms** (_neuro-anatomical bases_) underlying abstract human thought [19, 22]. McCulloch aimed to model complex Kantian concepts, such as the **synthetic judgment a priori** [23].

• **Concept:** Utilizing the advances of logicians like Gödel and Turing, they created a **network of propositions** (a precursor to formal neural networks) by connecting "atoms of thought" based on **Boolean logic** (0 or 1) [24-26].

• **Philosophical Stance:** McCulloch took the machine metaphor seriously, striving for a **realistic** description, not just a simulation [21, 25, 27]. He famously concluded: **"Plus nous apprenons des choses au sujet des organismes, plus nous sommes amenés à conclure qu'ils ne sont pas seulement analogues aux machines, mais qu'ils sont machines"** (The more we learn about organisms, the more we are led to conclude that they are not only analogous to machines, but that they _are_ machines) [20].

Cybernetics was foundational because it relied on **solid logical systems**, made **parallels with empirical sciences**, and introduced the idea of **spontaneous adaptation or auto-adjustment** based on signals (the feedback loop) [28, 29].

III. The Birth of AI and the Core Dualities (1956 onwards)

The term **Artificial Intelligence (IA)** was officially introduced at the **Dartmouth conference in 1956** [30]. It was defined as a research field seeking to **automate tasks traditionally reserved for the human intellect** [30].

This new field quickly split into two competing paradigms, often referred to pejoratively as the "Neats" versus the "Scruffys" [31, 32].

A key technical problem discussed is **Overfitting** (_surapprentissage_). This occurs when a model "memorizes" the training data. Overfitting is defined as the model finding a function that "sticks" to the training data, essentially leading to a form of rote learning (_apprentissage par cœur_). It is avoided by testing the model on new, unseen test data to verify its ability to generalize.

The central philosophical investigation asks whether it is truly justified to call these AI systems "models".

• **Cathy O'Neill's Perspective:** Drawing on her book _Weapons of Math Destruction_, the lecture cites O'Neill's definition of a model as an **"abstract representation of a calculated process"**. • **Model, Structure, and Formula (Bachelard):** Drawing on Gaston Bachelard's _Le matérialisme rationnel_, the discussion distinguishes the **model** from the **structure** and the **formula**.

    ◦ Bachelard argues against assigning excessive philosophical weight to the **structure**, which can lead to _chosisme_ (reifying concepts).

    ◦ Models (such as the electronic model of covalence or the quantum probabilistic model of the carbon atom) are preferred because they retain **dynamism** and **probability**, incorporating time and space, unlike a fixed structure.

    ◦ The **formula** is the synthetic expression (the final function, potentially with billions of parameters) that the estimator seeks to find. The model, particularly the neural network architecture, is the _process_ or _cheminement_ that arrives at this final formula.
